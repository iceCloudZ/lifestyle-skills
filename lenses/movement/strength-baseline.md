# Strength Baseline

Use this skill to establish a durable strength foundation with simple, repeatable training.

## Use When

- The person wants to get stronger.
- The person is new, returning, or aging-focused.
- The goal is baseline resilience rather than peak performance.

## Avoid When

- There is acute pain or unresolved injury.
- Technique support is unavailable for risky lifts.
- The person needs an advanced training cycle.

## Evidence Card

evidence_level: A
evidence_type: physical activity guidelines and strength-training practice
why_included: Basic strength work is a broadly supported health behavior, and this lens converts it into conservative beginner programming.
known_limits: It cannot check form, diagnose pain, or individualize around injuries without professional input.
safety_boundaries: Start conservatively, avoid pain, use simple movements, and recommend qualified coaching when technique or injury risk is unclear.
not_for: acute pain, unresolved injury, advanced programming, risky lifts without coaching, or medical restrictions

## Reasoning Flow

### 1. Pre-check: Should this lens be used here?

Use when the user wants baseline strength, resilience, or a simple return to training. If there is pain, injury, or medical restriction, hand off before programming.

### 2. Key assessment questions

- Is there current pain, injury history, or medical restriction?
- What equipment and time are available?
- Is the user new, returning, or already consistent?

### 3. Reasoning branches

- New or returning: choose low-risk movement patterns and very conservative volume.
- No equipment: use bodyweight squat, hinge, push, pull substitute, carry, and core options.
- Technique uncertainty: avoid complex lifts and suggest coaching or video guidance.
- Consistent baseline: progress one variable at a time.

### 4. Minimum complete answer

- Check or mention injury boundaries.
- Choose simple movement patterns.
- Give a conservative first session or movement menu.
- State how to stop or scale down if pain appears.

### 5. Hand-off conditions

Hand off for acute pain, neurological symptoms, injury rehab, medical restrictions, or technique-dependent heavy lifting.

## Thinking Steps

1. Check experience, injury history, equipment, and schedule.
2. Choose simple movement patterns: squat, hinge, push, pull, carry, core.
3. Start with conservative volume.
4. Progress one variable at a time.
5. Keep form and recovery ahead of load.

## Output Format

- Fit: why this lens applies.
- Baseline plan: simple movement menu.
- Safety note: what to avoid or watch.
- Next step: first session.

## Safety

Do not push through pain. Recommend qualified coaching for uncertain technique.
