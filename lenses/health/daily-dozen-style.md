# Daily Dozen Style

Use this skill to help a household improve nutrition quality through a simple food checklist lens.

## Use When

- The person asks how to eat healthier day to day.
- The household wants practical grocery or meal upgrades.
- A checklist is motivating rather than stressful.

## Avoid When

- There is eating disorder risk or obsessive tracking.
- A clinician has prescribed a specific diet.
- Allergies or medical constraints require professional guidance.

## Evidence Card

evidence_level: B
evidence_type: nutrition guideline alignment plus plant-forward checklist practice
why_included: This lens turns broad healthy-eating guidance into small additive choices around legumes, whole grains, fruits, vegetables, nuts, and seeds.
known_limits: The specific checklist is not a personalized diet and may be too rigid for some users.
safety_boundaries: Add before subtracting. Avoid weight-loss prescriptions, medical diets, and obsessive tracking.
not_for: eating disorder risk, complex allergies, clinician-prescribed diets, or medical nutrition therapy

## Reasoning Flow

### 1. Pre-check: Should this lens be used here?

Use when the user wants everyday meal quality improvements and a checklist feels supportive. Avoid when tracking creates anxiety or medical constraints dominate.

### 2. Key assessment questions

- Which meal or grocery moment is easiest to improve?
- Which plant-forward category is already acceptable?
- What cooking, budget, allergy, or family constraint matters?

### 3. Reasoning branches

- Takeout-heavy: add one produce, bean, or whole-grain side before changing the whole meal.
- Grocery planning: pick one repeatable item from a missing category.
- Family resistance: make one low-friction swap rather than a strict rule.
- Tracking anxiety: use a loose "add one" cue instead of a checklist.

### 4. Minimum complete answer

- Suggest an additive food choice.
- Keep the swap realistic for the user's capacity.
- Avoid prescribing weight loss or a medical diet.
- Give one meal or shopping action.

### 5. Hand-off conditions

Hand off for eating disorder risk, medical nutrition therapy, severe allergies, pregnancy-specific nutrition, or clinician-directed diets.

## Thinking Steps

1. Identify current meal pattern and constraints.
2. Add before subtracting when possible.
3. Prioritize legumes, whole grains, fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds, and spices where appropriate.
4. Keep swaps realistic for cooking capacity and budget.
5. Suggest one next meal or grocery choice.

## Output Format

- Fit: why this lens applies.
- Add: one food group to increase.
- Swap: one realistic substitution.
- Next step: one meal or shopping action.

## Safety

Do not prescribe diets for medical conditions or weight-loss treatment.
